Symbolic Solutions to Differential Equations
Campos H.∗, Montero E.∗∗

Abstract
While traditional numerical methods provide reliable discrete approximations for complex differential
equations, they fail to yield closed-form mathematical expressions. Recent physics-informed machine
learning approaches, such as PINNs, attempt to address this but frequently suffer from optimization
instabilities due to weighted penalty functions and a lack of mathematical interpretability. This study
introduces the Physics-Informed Symbolic Regression Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II
(PISR-NSGA-II)., a hybrid Physics-Informed Multi-Objective Symbolic Regression framework for
discovering exact analytical solutions to partial differential equations (PDEs). Unlike traditional
methods, PISR-NSGA-II formulates the search as a strictly tri-objective Pareto optimization problem, independently minimizing domain residuals, boundary condition errors, and expression complexity, thereby eliminating the need for manual penalty weights. Utilizing an object-oriented tree
representation, the algorithm enables exact Automated Symbolic Differentiation (AD), bypassing the
computational overhead of finite differences. Evaluated across a comprehensive benchmark suite of
13 linear and non-linear PDEs, the framework exhibited a bifurcated performance profile. For systems with exact closed-form solutions, PISR-NSGA-II achieved machine-precision accuracy (10−30 to
10−33 ) and successfully reconstructed complex formulations, including the 2D Navier-Stokes stream
function. However, for PDEs lacking simple analytical forms that rely on numerical approximations,
the algorithm struggles to compress complex numerical fields into parsimonious symbolic trees, resulting in higher residual errors (10−1 to 10−3 ). Despite this limitation in numerical field approximation,
the proposed framework demonstrates significant advantages over deep learning baselines in both
computational efficiency and mathematical interpretability for exactly solvable systems.

Optimization, Multi-Objective

Many complex phenomena in physics and engineering are governed by differential equations Tsoulos and Lagaris (2006). While traditional numerical algorithms, such as the Runge-Kutta method
Runge (1895); Kutta (1901), provide reliable discrete approximations Mateescu (2006), they do
not yield closed-form mathematical expressions. For many complex Partial Differential Equations
(PDEs), exact closed-form analytical solutions are unavailable Fish and Belytschko (2007); Morton
and Mayers (2012). To address these limitations, meta-heuristic algorithms and symbolic optimiza∗

In recent years, the integration of prior physical knowledge into optimization models has gained
significant traction, extending the principles of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) Raissi

population-based search, and requires extensive manual hyperparameter tuning, which frequently
compromises the stability and accuracy of the convergence.
To overcome these limitations, this study introduces the Physics-Informed Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (PISR-NSGA-II) for the exact symbolic resolution of partial differential
equations. Unlike traditional physics-informed models, PISR-NSGA-II evaluates domain residuals
and boundary requirements as strictly independent objectives within a bi-objective Pareto framework, completely eliminating manual penalty weights. Furthermore, by utilizing an object-oriented,
tree-based chromosome representation embedded with Ephemeral Random Constants (ERC) Koza
(1992), our framework enables native, exact Automated Symbolic Differentiation (AD) Baydin et al.
(2018). This design bypasses the computational overhead of finite differences or external heavy autodifferentiation libraries, allowing the simultaneous evolution of functional topology and numerical
coefficients to discover robust, closed-form analytical solutions with mathematical precision.

procedures, while Section 6 presents the numerical results and comparative analyses.
2. Related Work
The application of meta-heuristic search strategies to differential equations has evolved significantly, bridging the gap between flexible symbolic regression and physics-driven optimization. This
section reviews existing approaches, categorizing them into pure evolutionary methods for differential
equations and hybrid physics-informed meta-heuristics.
2.1. Evolutionary Algorithms for Differential Equations
Early paradigms heavily relied on grammar-based structures or specialized mathematical polynomials to construct candidate solutions. Tsoulos and Lagaris (2006) introduced Grammatical Evolution (GE) for solving differential equations, utilizing a codified Backus-Naur Form (BNF) where
chromosomes are represented as integer arrays mapping to sequences of derivation rules. Although
effective at discovering analytical expressions, this methodology is computationally intensive due to
its reliance on finite difference methods for derivative evaluation.
To refine parameter transitions and ensure smooth solutions, Albadarneh et al. (2021) adapted the
Continuous Genetic Algorithm (CGA) originally developed by Abo-Hammour et al. (2010) for PDEs.
In their approach, each chromosome is a real-valued vector representing discrete solution values over
a spatial grid, optimizing nodal approximations via a finite difference scheme Richardson (1911).
Similarly, Abo-bakr et al. (2022) proposed a technique based on Bernstein polynomials Bernstein

2

(1912); Lorentz (1986), using a hybrid Particle Swarm Optimization and Genetic Algorithm (PSOGA) scheme to minimize the sum of squared residuals in high-order non-linear ordinary and partial
differential equations.
More recently, Genetic Programming has been enhanced by incorporating the differential equation
as a regularizer within the objective function. Utilizing a symbolic representation, Oh et al. (2023)
and Cohen (1988) demonstrated that allowing the algorithm to explore a diverse functional space of
trial functions significantly improves convergence toward stable analytical solutions from scarce or
noisy data.
2.2. Physics-Informed Meta-heuristics
Integrating physical laws directly into meta-heuristics has emerged as a powerful mechanism to
constrain search spaces and accelerate learning without relying on gradient-based optimization. Du
et al. (2022) developed a data-driven Evolutionary Strategy (ES) incorporating a physics-informed
Trainable Action Mask (TAM), which applies prior physical knowledge to filter improper control
actions, improving exploration efficiency in high-dimensional spaces. Similarly, Zhang (2024) introduced the AI-enhanced multi-scale physics-informed optimization algorithm (AMPHIS) framework,
utilizing a multi-scale particle representation coupled with a deep learning guide and a self-correction
physical system that projects particles into feasible physical regions.
To demonstrate the efficacy of physics-informed initializations, Papadopoulos et al. (2024) embedded geometric principles into meta-heuristic algorithms (MAs), comparing standard frameworks
against their physics-informed counterparts. Their empirical results across multiple algorithmic
engines—including the Gradient Descent Algorithm (GDA), traditional GA, Non-Dominated Sorting
Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) Deb et al. (2002), and Strength Pareto Evolutionary Algorithm 2
(SPEA2) Amuso and Enslin (2007)—demonstrated that incorporating physical domain constraints
consistently yields superior optimization performance.
Expanding the structural design of physics-inspired operators, Liu et al. (2026) proposed the
Cyclotron Accelerator Optimization Framework (CAOF), embedding magnetic-field deflection and
electric-field acceleration operators into the Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO) Mirjalili et al. (2014) to
achieve a dynamic balance between global exploration and local exploitation. Furthermore, Ouyang
et al. (2026) proposed Physics-informed Evolution (PIE), extending the principles of PINNs directly
into evolutionary computation. By embedding physical information from governing equations into
the fitness landscape, the multi-objective PIE formulation successfully reveals Pareto-optimal tradeoffs between task performance and physics-informed regularization costs, proving that gradient-free
evolutionary search can be effectively governed by underlying physical laws.
2.3. Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
In parallel with evolutionary methods, Deep Learning has revolutionized the field of scientific
computing through the introduction of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al.,
2019b). Instead of representing solutions symbolically, PINNs parameterize the unknown function
u(x) using a deep neural network, embedding the governing differential equations directly into the
loss function as regularizing terms evaluated at a set of collocation points. This framework has
been widely extended to solve both forward and inverse problems across diverse physical domains,
including fluid dynamics, heat transfer, and solid mechanics (Karniadakis et al., 2021).
To standardize the implementation of these neural solvers, several open-source libraries have been
developed, most notably DeepXDE (Lu et al., 2021), which provides a unified interface for solving
partial differential equations on complex geometries using multiple deep learning backends. Despite
their success in high-dimensional domains, PINNs suffer from well-documented limitations, such as
training instabilities stemming from the non-convex optimization landscape of multi-objective loss
3

functions (e.g., balancing boundary and domain residuals), lack of mathematical interpretability,
and significant computational overhead during training due to standard backpropagation-based gradient calculations (Cuomo et al., 2022). These limitations have motivated the search for alternative
frameworks, such as the multi-objective symbolic regression approach proposed in this work.
Recently, Oh et al. (2023) demonstrated that Genetic Programming-based Symbolic Regression
(GPSR) can successfully recover exact analytical solutions to differential equations by incorporating
physics-regularized fitness functions. However, their framework relies on translating syntax trees into
external automatic differentiation libraries (such as PyTorch) to compute the necessary gradients.
As the authors noted, this approach introduces a severe computational penalty, rendering the algorithmic evaluation significantly slower than conventional function evaluations. Furthermore, their
methodology aggregates domain residuals and boundary conditions into a single scalar fitness metric,
inherently reintroducing the optimization vulnerabilities associated with penalty weight tuning.
To directly address these critical limitations, the proposed PISR-NSGA-II framework completely
bypasses external deep learning libraries by embedding exact Automated Symbolic Differentiation
(AD) natively within the object-oriented syntax tree nodes. Coupled with a strictly multi-objective
Pareto formulation that evaluates domain and boundary residuals independently, this architectural
design enables the highly efficient and robust discovery of complex, non-linear analytical solutions
without sacrificing computational speed or boundary condition integrity.

4. Proposed Physics-Informed Evolutionary Algorithm
To discover the closed-form analytical solutions of complex differential equations, we propose a
hybrid multi-objective evolutionary framework based on Symbolic Regression. Building upon the
recent formalization of Physics-Informed Genetic Programming (PIGP) (Cohen et al., 2024), our
architecture utilizes a tree-based representation to dynamically explore functional spaces.
Unlike conventional genetic programming, our approach introduces three major algorithmic enhancements:
1. Tri-Objective Optimization: We formulate the search as a three-objective optimization
problem, explicitly separating the domain residual, the boundary condition error, and the tree
complexity (parsimony pressure) to naturally control bloat.
2. Hybrid Local Search: We integrate a second-order Gauss-Newton (GN) local optimizer
directly into the evolutionary loop. This solver uses QR decomposition of the Jacobian matrix
to fine-tune continuous Ephemeral Random Constants (ERCs) at the leaf level, complementing
global genetic search with rapid local convergence.
3. Boundary-Conforming Seeds: We inject physically motivated boundary-conforming trees
(ansatzes) into the initial population, jump-starting the evolutionary search with high-fitness
individuals that exactly or approximately satisfy boundary states.
4.1. Tree-Based Chromosome Representation and Polynomial Collapse
Following the standard Genetic Programming paradigm established by Koza (1992), our approach abandons fixed-length numerical arrays in favor of a dynamic, object-oriented syntax tree
representation. This allows the algorithm to evolve equations of varying length and complexity.
Each gene in the population is implemented as a node structure in memory, classified into two
categories:
• Terminal Nodes (Leaves): Located at the bottom of the tree, these nodes provide the fundamental inputs for the equation. They represent either independent spatial/temporal variables
(e.g., x, y, t) or Ephemeral Random Constants (ERCs) representing continuous parameters.
• Internal Nodes (Operators): These nodes encapsulate mathematical functions (e.g., +, −,
×, sin, exp) and specialized orthogonal function nodes, namely Legendre (Pn (x)) and Hermite
(Hn (x)) polynomials.
For Legendre and Hermite polynomial operators, we implement a binary structure where the left
child represents the input argument x and the right child represents the constant degree constraint
n ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3}.
To provide a clearer understanding of this computational architecture, Figure 1 illustrates the
internal memory structure of a candidate tree-based chromosome. In this specific example, the
tree encodes the mathematical expression û(x, y) = 4.5 sin(x) + y 2 , demonstrating the hierarchical
relationship between operators, spatial variables, and ERCs.
4.2. Recursive Evaluation and Automated Symbolic Differentiation

recursive post-order traversal of the expression tree. The evaluation proceeds from the bottom up,
replacing terminal variables with the numerical coordinates of the collocation point and applying the
operator nodes sequentially.

4.5. Boundary-Conforming Physics-Informed Initializations
To accelerate search in highly constrained physical domains, we seed the initial population with
boundary-conforming functional ansatzes. Instead of starting from entirely random trees that violate
boundary conditions, we inject approximate or exact physical seeds at generation 0:
• Fisher/Duffing/Thomas-Fermi 2D: Linear boundary profiles of the form u(x, y) = c0 +
c1 (x + y), matching the exact boundary values prescribed at the rectangular boundaries.
• Airy 1D/2D: Exponential decay profiles u(x, y) = c0 e−c1 x matching the characteristic decay
of the Airy function Ai(x) on [0, 1].
• Thomas-Fermi 1D: Non-linear exponential ansatzes u(x) = e−c1 x satisfying u(0) = 1 and
u(1) = 0.2.
These seed structures provide high-fitness templates that guide the crossover and mutation operators
toward boundary-consistent search space regions.

5. Experimental Methodology and Evaluation Metrics
To rigorously evaluate the quality, physical consistency, and efficiency of the symbolic solutions
discovered by the proposed PI-NSGA-II framework against the established baselines, we employ five
primary performance metrics:
• Domain Residual (MSE LΩ ): Quantifies the mean squared error of the discovered analytical
expression û with respect to the governing differential operator F evaluated across the interior
collocation points. This metric serves as the primary indicator of adherence to the underlying
physical laws.
• Boundary Error (MSE L∂Ω ): Measures the deviation of the candidate solution from the
prescribed initial or boundary conditions B. Strict minimization of this metric is critical to
ensure that the discovered PDE solution is unique, physically consistent, and mathematically
well-posed.
• Hypervolume (HV): Since the proposed framework employs a Pareto-based multi-objective
optimization strategy, HV is utilized to measure the volume of the objective space (Domain vs.
Boundary error) strictly dominated by the obtained Pareto front. It serves as a comprehensive
indicator of algorithmic convergence and the quality of the trade-off solutions.
• Expression Complexity (C(û)): Evaluates the structural size of the symbolic tree (total node
count). Tracking complexity is essential to monitor parsimony pressure, prevent equation bloat,
and ensure that the final mathematical formulations remain elegant and human-interpretable.
• Computational Runtime: The total wall-clock time required to achieve convergence. This
metric assesses the execution efficiency of the native Automated Symbolic Differentiation (AD)
embedded in the object-oriented trees, especially when compared against the backpropagation
overhead of DeepXDE.

• Computational Efficiency: PISR-NSGA-II completes its structural search and parameter
optimization in under a minute for most PDEs. For analytical cases, the boundary-conforming
seeding strategy combined with the local Gauss-Newton optimizer enables immediate convergence at generation 0. Conversely, DeepXDE requires backpropagation-based gradient descent over
thousands of collocation points on deep neural architectures, resulting in long training runtimes
(exceeding 1.5 hours per 2D equation on GPU).
• Exact Symbolic Reconstruction: Table 4 demonstrates that PISR-NSGA-II successfully
reconstructs the exact target mathematical formulations. For instance, in the 2D steady NavierStokes Kovasznay flow problem, PISR-NSGA-II accurately discovered the stream function profile

which matches the analytical solution (where 2πRe ≈ 125.6637 and λ ≈ −1.8101).
• Boundary Condition Satisfaction: Traditional physics-informed algorithms often compromise boundary integrity to minimize domain residuals. Under the tri-objective Pareto formulation of PISR-NSGA-II, the boundary condition residual (f2 ) is treated as an independent

constraints exactly, yielding boundary errors of 10−32 (e.g., in 1D Fisher) and preventing the
boundary leakage issues common in neural network baselines.
• Numerical Reference Comparison (RK4/FDM): For equations lacking closed-form analytical solutions (Airy, Fisher, Duffing, and Thomas-Fermi), the 1D/2D numerical solutions
computed via Runge-Kutta 4th order and Finite Difference relaxation serve as the Ground
Truth (represented as (0.00 ± 0.00) × 100 error in Table 5). Both PISR-NSGA-II and DeepXDE
solutions were evaluated against these discrete reference grids, with PISR-NSGA-II obtaining
highly accurate symbolic approximations (e.g., 10−3 in 1D Airy) that capture the physical
properties of the underlying fields.

7. Conclusions
This paper presented PISR-NSGA-II, a novel physics-informed symbolic regression framework
designed to discover closed-form analytical solutions to complex differential equations. By restructuring the learning process into a tri-objective Pareto optimization problem, the framework successfully
decoupled domain accuracy, boundary compliance, and equation complexity. This formulation eliminated the pervasive vulnerabilities associated with manual penalty weights, ensuring that candidate
solutions strictly satisfied physical boundary states.
The integration of exact Automated Symbolic Differentiation (AD) within an object-oriented
syntax tree proved computationally superior to standard neural network backpropagation. Furthermore, hybridizing the evolutionary engine with a local second-order Gauss-Newton solver successfully
bridged the gap between global topological search and local parameter optimization.
Empirical evaluations across a rigorous 13-PDE benchmark suite revealed a clear, bifurcated
performance profile. For equations with exact closed-form analytical solutions, PISR-NSGA-II consistently operates at machine-precision accuracy (10−30 to 10−33 ), autonomously reconstructing complex non-linear functional forms such as the 2D Navier-Stokes stream function. However, the study
also highlights a fundamental limitation of the symbolic approach: when applied to highly non-linear
PDEs lacking compact analytical solutions (e.g., Duffing, Fisher, and Thomas-Fermi), where the true
physics require complex numerical approximations or infinite series, the algorithm struggles. Forcing
a purely numerical field into a parsimonious symbolic tree restricts the solution manifold, resulting
in higher residual errors (10−1 to 10−3 ) and sub-optimal hypervolume convergence.
Despite these challenges in pure numerical approximation, for systems governed by definable analytical expressions, the symbolic approach consistently outperformed deep learning baselines (PINNs)
by several orders of magnitude in convergence speed and residual error.
